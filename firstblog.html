The Media Made You Abandon Skepticism

Introduction

This blog dives into the profound influence media has on shaping not only how we think but also how we question and feel. From the headlines we click to the stories we share, media doesn’t merely inform us, it influences us. But how often do we pause to critically examine what we’re consuming and why it matters? Let’s explore the subtle yet significant ways media impacts our thinking and mental well-being and why cultivating skepticism is more important than ever.

What is Skepticism, and Why Does It Matter?

Skepticism is the practice of critically questioning the validity of information before accepting it as truth. It’s not about rejecting everything outright but about approaching ideas with a discerning eye, supported by evidence and reason. Why does this matter? Because seeking truth is foundational not only for staying informed but for engaging in meaningful conversations and avoiding manipulation.

Personally, I wasn’t always skeptical. I used to take almost everything I heard, read, or saw at face value. Whether it was from prominent news outlets like ABC, MSNBC, or FOX, or later on social media platforms like Instagram, Facebook, and TikTok, I often absorbed information uncritically. Hours of scrolling led me to consume content passively, watching users echo the same narratives over and over.

At some point, I realized I wasn’t questioning anything—I was simply believing. The turning point came when I noticed the intensity of emotional reactions both in myself and others. Anger, outrage, and fear were everywhere, but I started to wonder: What are these feelings rooted in? Are they responses to truth, or just to what feels true? This was the beginning of my journey toward skepticism and critical thinking.

Why Don’t We Question What We Hear?

One reason many people avoid questioning information is the overwhelming sense of having too much on their plates—what some might call a lack of cognitive emotional equilibrium. Between work, family, and personal responsibilities, the mental effort required to fact-check or dig deeper often feels like a luxury. But this avoidance comes at a cost: it leaves us vulnerable to misinformation and emotional manipulation.

Critical thinking isn’t an innate skill; it’s learned through deliberate practice. Just as mastering subjects like math or history required repetition and effort, cultivating skepticism takes time and commitment. Yet, considering the impact this has on our understanding of the world, isn’t it worth the investment?

The Role of Confirmation Bias

Another significant barrier to skepticism is confirmation bias—our natural tendency to seek out and accept information that reinforces our existing beliefs while ignoring or dismissing evidence to the contrary. It’s comfortable and validating, but this habit often prevents us from seeing the full picture.

Instead of challenging our assumptions, we retreat into echo chambers, both online and offline, that insulate us from opposing perspectives. While this can provide short-term comfort, it can also blind us to truths that might challenge or enrich our understanding. Recognizing this bias is a critical step toward developing the kind of skepticism that promotes clarity, growth, and resilience.
